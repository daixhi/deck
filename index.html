26 Core Facts

1. Central isomorphism ↳ Seen in "First Principles"

Same five-stage pipeline run against a different document type per docket: ingest → typed schema → agentic reasoning → evidence graph → sign-off. Courtroom runs it on case files, PubSec on government filings, Audit on ledgers and Form 26. Pipeline shape is constant. Document type changes.

2. Category of improvement / whose experience ↳ Seen in "Execution Model"

  • Category of improvement: Eliminates the manual drudgery of verification, reconciliation, and cross-referencing. The engine compresses weeks of manual ticking and tying into minutes, shifting human effort entirely from data gathering to professional judgment.
  • Whose experience is improved: The entire liability chain. Analysts skip line-by-line manual matching. Managers review surfaced exceptions instead of re-reading raw files. The Signing CA assumes statutory liability backed by pixel-precise lineage, not blind trust.
  • How roles correspond: The architecture maps 1:1 across dockets because the chain of liability is mathematically identical. Jhana's Courtroom permission tiers (Paralegal → Clerk → Judge) translate directly into the Audit hierarchy (Analyst → Manager → Signing CA).

3. Type / role / ontology / temporality ↳ Seen in "Systemic advantage" & "Evidence Graph"

  • Type: Ingestion converts chaotic text into strongly-typed primitives (Invoice_Date, Vendor_GSTIN, PAN, TransactionAmount). The engine template-matches standard software layouts deterministically, deploying AI vision only when a messy, unstructured format breaks the rules.
  • Role: Assigns entities dynamic, relational functions based on the accounting context (e.g., distinguishing a standard vendor from a Related Party to filter search queries). Natively, it maps permissions to a true human audit hierarchy (Analyst → Associate → Manager → Signing CA) to preserve the legal chain of liability
  • Ontology: Translates the rigid tax code and Rule 47 framework into an interconnected database schema. Instead of flat keyword searches, it constructs an Evidence Graph that directly anchors financial line items to their exact statutory obligations (e.g., mapping loan schedules to Form 26 Clause 45).
  • Temporality: Normalizes messy dates into uniform ISO timestamps to build chronological lifecycles across separate data streams. It computes temporal math to automatically flag statutory cutoff violations, timing mismatches (like the 180-day ITC rule), or post-balance-sheet anomalies.

4. GPAIs vs. jhana.ai ↳ Seen in "Systemic Advantage" & "Execution Model"

Localized LLMs (and propreitory models like Codex) remain fundamentally prompt-response systems. They retrieve semantically similar text through vector search, reason within a finite context window, generate a single response, and return plain text. jhana.ai replaces each of those assumptions. Instead of similarity search, it traverses a legal ontology that models entities, statutory relationships, and evidentiary links, allowing retrieval based on legal structure rather than lexical proximity. Documents are evaluated as a connected body of evidence through multi-hop timeline construction, enabling contradictions to be detected across files. A reflective agent pipeline decomposes complex work into specialised stages, validates intermediate artifacts against source material, and iterates before producing a final output. Outputs are generated through a native DOCX pipeline, preserving legal formatting, tracked changes, citations, and filing-ready structure rather than unstructured markdown.

5. Bespoke to tax & FDE deliverables ↳ Seen in "First Principles" & "Execution Model"

  • Bespoke to Tax: Tax audits are constrained by statutory templates and arithmetic precision. Responses cannot be drafted as conversational text; they must reconcile millions of rows across ledgers, bank statements, GST 2A/2B, and 26AS. Large parts of the deliverable have exactly one correct mathematical output.
  • The Execution Split: Audit solves this by splitting the workload. AI handles judgment and narrative, while a net-new programmatic track (Track 3A) performs all reconciliation deterministically as code. Pushing numeric computation through an LLM is an anti-pattern; our split eliminates hallucination risk where accuracy is non-negotiable.
  • What the FDE Builds: The Forward-Deployed Engineer (FDE) doesn't just install a wrapper. They build the dedicated arithmetic layer (Track 3A), map the firm's actual chart of accounts, wire the engine to live GSTN and TRACES feeds, and map the rigid Form 26 clause ontology.

6. GTM advantage with open-weights ↳ Seen in "Systemic Advantage"

  • Owning the Stack: Frontier API providers sell the reasoning layer, leaving clients to build ingestion, ontology, security, and integration, locked to one model's limits. We own the stack below the model.
  • Swappable Infrastructure: Open-weights makes the model swappable and self-hosted (waterfalling across Mistral/Groq/Gemini/Cerebras) with dead-model failover.
  • The Economic & Security Moat: The result is that data never leaves the client's VPC, there is zero dependency on frontier APIs, and token costs are vastly lower. Competitors cannot match this architecture without cutting into their own model margins.

! 3. Form 26 Domain Caveats

Form No. 26 Regime Status

Brand-new & untested: Section 63, Income-tax Act 2025 / Rule 47, Income-tax Rules 2026, first applicable FY starting 1 Apr 2026. No filed precedent, no case law, no market-standard interpretation yet.

Entire vertical is a bet on a regime still being settled — not "indexing an established form" like 3CA/3CB/3CD.

Part-Lettering Conflict

FAQ vs Form Template: FAQ 8 assigns lettering (A=assessee, B=Statement of Particulars). The form's own printed headers run the reverse.

Resolution: The pitch follows the form's own printed header (Part D = Statement of Particulars). Whichever lettering the e-filing schema enforces at implementation is what will actually matter.
